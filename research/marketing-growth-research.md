# Marketing, Growth & Promotion Taste: Research Report for Vibe Coders

## Executive Summary

This report covers 17 marketing and growth areas essential for indie hackers and solo builders (vibe coders) who want to develop marketing "taste" -- the intuition for what converts, what resonates, and what grows a product organically. Each section includes principles, real-world examples of good vs. bad marketing, and concrete exercises a vibe coder can complete in 30-90 minutes.

---

## 1. Landing Page Design Best Practices

### What Converts

- **Clear hero section**: Visitors decide in under 3 seconds whether to stay. The hero must answer: Who is this for? What does it do? What's the key benefit?
- **Single CTA per page**: Pages with one focused call-to-action convert significantly higher than those with multiple competing CTAs
- **Social proof above the fold**: Logos, testimonials, or user counts build immediate trust
- **Mobile-first design**: 82.9% of landing page traffic is mobile; mobile-responsive pages convert at ~11.7%
- **Page speed**: Pages loading in 1 second convert 300% more than those loading in 5 seconds
- **Personalized CTAs**: Convert 202% better than generic ones

### What Doesn't Convert

- Vague headlines like "Get more done!" or "Increase collaboration!" without explaining what the product actually does
- Feature dumps without connecting features to user outcomes
- Multiple CTAs competing for attention (sign up, watch demo, read blog, follow on Twitter)
- Missing problem statement -- skipping over the pain point that drives users to seek a solution
- Walls of text with no visual hierarchy
- Stock photos that feel generic and impersonal

### Good vs. Bad Examples

**Good**: Basecamp's landing page leads with the specific problem ("Before Basecamp: Projects feel scattered, things slip, it's tough to see where things stand"). Then it shows the solution with real screenshots.

**Bad**: A generic SaaS page that says "The #1 Platform for Teams" with no explanation of what it does, who it's for, or why anyone should care. Pages that list "save time and money", "get faster insights", "increase sales" but forget to explain HOW.

### Hero Section Framework (3x Conversion)
1. **Who** the solution is for
2. **What** your product does (concretely)
3. **Key benefit** / transformation
4. Add a Problem Agitation block after the hero (describe users' problem and negative consequences)
5. Describe features through their benefits
6. Connect price to transformation

### Exercise 1: Landing Page Teardown (45 min)
Pick 5 successful indie hacker products from Indie Hackers or Product Hunt. For each:
- Screenshot the above-the-fold section
- Identify: headline clarity (1-10), CTA strength, social proof presence, load time (use PageSpeed Insights)
- Write down what you'd change and why
- Compare your notes with the actual conversion data if available

### Exercise 2: Write 5 Hero Sections (60 min)
For your product (or a hypothetical one):
- Write 5 different hero section variations using different angles: pain-focused, benefit-focused, curiosity-driven, social-proof-led, and feature-led
- Ask 3 people which one makes them want to learn more
- A/B test the top 2 on your actual landing page

### Exercise 3: Speed Audit Challenge (30 min)
Run your landing page (or 3 competitor pages) through Google PageSpeed Insights, GTmetrix, and WebPageTest. Document:
- Load time on mobile vs. desktop
- Largest Contentful Paint (LCP) score
- Three specific fixes you could implement today

## 2. SEO Basics for Non-Technical People

### Core Principles

- **Keyword research**: Find words people type when searching for solutions like yours. Use Google Keyword Planner, Ubersuggest, or Ahrefs free tools
- **Content quality**: Google favors human, experience-driven content over AI-generated mass-produced articles
- **Technical foundation**: SSL certificate (https), XML sitemap, fast page speed, mobile-first design
- **Server-side rendering (SSR)**: Critical for SEO; Google generally struggles to parse JavaScript-heavy sites
- **OG tags**: Meta tags (og:title, og:description, og:image) that make links look better when shared on social media
- **Long-tail keywords**: Less competition, more targeted traffic, higher conversion rates

### What Works

- Writing articles that answer specific questions your target users are Googling
- Building backlinks by providing genuine value (guest posts, tools, research)
- Targeting long-tail keywords with buyer intent ("best project management tool for freelancers" vs. "project management")
- Consistent publishing schedule with quality over quantity

### What Doesn't Work

- Keyword stuffing or thin content
- Expecting immediate results (SEO is a 6-12 month game)
- Ignoring technical SEO basics
- Trading backlinks (Google penalizes this)
- Using SEO as your only channel when you need fast validation

### Good vs. Bad Examples

**Good**: An indie hacker building a code review tool writes detailed blog posts like "How to do code reviews in a team of 2" -- targeting a specific long-tail keyword with genuine expertise.

**Bad**: An indie hacker writes 50 AI-generated blog posts stuffed with keywords and no original insights. Google deprioritizes them, and the content provides no value to readers.

### Exercise 4: Keyword Research Sprint (45 min)
- Use Google's "People Also Ask" and autocomplete for your product niche
- List 20 long-tail keywords your ideal customers might search
- Check search volume and competition using Ubersuggest (free tier)
- Pick the top 3 keywords with decent volume and low competition
- Outline one blog post for each

### Exercise 5: Technical SEO Checklist (30 min)
Run through this checklist for your site:
- [ ] SSL certificate installed (https)
- [ ] XML sitemap created and submitted to Google Search Console
- [ ] OG tags present on all pages
- [ ] Mobile responsive
- [ ] Page loads under 3 seconds
- [ ] No broken links (use a free broken link checker)
- [ ] robots.txt configured properly

## 3. Product Hunt Launch Strategies

### What Works

- **Pre-launch audience building**: Build your audience weeks/months before launch. Reach out to influencers, potential users, and first-degree connections
- **Personalized outreach**: LinkedIn and direct contacts outperform mass emails and generic community posts
- **Multi-platform approach**: Don't rely solely on Product Hunt. Also submit to BetaList, Indie Hackers, Hacker News, and niche directories
- **Transparency**: If your product is an MVP, say so. People respect honesty
- **Engagement**: Reply to every single comment on your Product Hunt page
- **Quality visuals**: Good screenshots, clean logo, professional-looking product

### Common Mistakes

- Overthinking and over-preparing (some teams push too hard and damage their brands)
- Asking people directly for upvotes (violates Product Hunt guidelines and feels spammy)
- Relying solely on Product Hunt for distribution
- Blurry screenshots or clipart-quality logos
- Ignoring comments on launch day
- Believing that visibility equals viability -- a Product Hunt spike doesn't mean sustainable growth

### Good vs. Bad Examples

**Good**: A founder spends 3 weeks before launch personally messaging 200 people who had expressed interest in the problem space. On launch day, they respond to every comment within 15 minutes. They also post on 5 other directories the same week.

**Bad**: A founder launches cold with no pre-built audience, sends a mass email blast saying "Please upvote my product!", and doesn't respond to comments until the next day.

### Exercise 6: Product Hunt Research (60 min)
- Study the top 5 Product Hunt launches of the past month
- For each, analyze: thumbnail quality, tagline, first comment (maker's intro), number of comments and engagement, what platforms they cross-posted to
- Write a mock Product Hunt launch plan for your product including: pre-launch timeline, outreach list (20 people minimum), tagline options, first comment draft

### Exercise 7: Directory Submission Sprint (45 min)
Find and list 15 directories, communities, and platforms where you could submit your product. For each, note:
- Submission requirements
- Expected traffic/SEO benefit
- Whether they're free or paid
- Schedule submissions across 2 weeks (don't launch everywhere simultaneously)

## 4. Social Media Marketing for Software Products

### Platform Strategy

- **Twitter/X**: Best for building in public, short updates, engaging with other founders. The indie hacker community lives here
- **LinkedIn**: Best for B2B products, daily insights, and personalized messages for feedback
- **Reddit**: High-intent users searching for solutions. Provide genuine value before promoting
- **YouTube/TikTok**: Short-form video is increasingly important; tutorials and demos perform well

### What Works

- Content that entertains, educates, or informs (over 60% of social content now aims to entertain)
- Sharing authentic journey updates (revenue milestones, lessons learned, failures)
- Engaging in conversations rather than just broadcasting
- Repurposing content across platforms (one idea becomes a tweet thread, LinkedIn post, short video, and blog post)

### What Doesn't Work

- Pure self-promotion without providing value
- Posting inconsistently
- Trying to be on every platform simultaneously
- Using the same format/tone across all platforms
- Automation that feels robotic

### Good vs. Bad Examples

**Good**: An indie hacker shares a thread: "I just hit $5K MRR. Here's every mistake I made in the first 6 months..." -- provides genuine value while naturally promoting their product.

**Bad**: An indie hacker posts "Check out my new app! [link]" repeatedly across 10 subreddits with no context or value-add.

### Exercise 8: Content Repurposing Chain (60 min)
Take one insight, lesson, or feature from your product and create:
1. A tweet / X post (under 280 characters)
2. A Twitter/X thread (5-7 tweets expanding the idea)
3. A LinkedIn post (professional angle, 150-300 words)
4. A Reddit comment/post (value-first, no direct promotion)
5. A short video script (60 seconds)
Compare how tone and framing shift across platforms.

### Exercise 9: Competitor Social Audit (45 min)
Pick 3 competitors or similar products. For each:
- What platforms are they active on?
- What's their posting frequency?
- Which posts got the most engagement? Why?
- What content format do they use most?
- What gap could you fill that they're not covering?

## 5. Content Marketing and Developer Relations

### Core Principles

- Content marketing is the go-to strategy for bootstrapped startups because it's sustainable long-term (unlike paid ads)
- Google, LinkedIn, and Twitter are favoring human, experience-driven content over AI-generated mass content
- People want insights, stories, and unique takes -- not reworded Wikipedia entries
- Building authority through genuine expertise is the most durable strategy

### What Works

- Blog posts that solve specific problems your users face
- Tutorials showing how to use your product for real-world tasks
- Case studies of actual users getting results
- Open-source contributions and developer tools
- Conference talks and podcast appearances
- Documentation that doubles as content marketing

### What Doesn't Work

- Generic blog posts that could apply to any product
- Content mills producing low-quality volume
- Ignoring SEO entirely in content strategy
- Writing for search engines instead of humans
- Not distributing content after publishing (the "publish and pray" approach)

### Good vs. Bad Examples

**Good**: Plausible Analytics writes detailed blog posts comparing themselves to Google Analytics on specific features, targeting people searching for "Google Analytics alternatives." Each post provides genuine insight into privacy-focused analytics.

**Bad**: A developer tool company publishes 30 blog posts titled "Why [Product] is the Best" with no useful information, just self-congratulatory marketing speak.

### Exercise 10: Content Calendar Sprint (45 min)
- List 10 questions your target users frequently ask (check Reddit, Twitter, StackOverflow, forums)
- For each question, outline a blog post that provides a genuinely helpful answer
- Plan a 4-week publishing schedule (2 posts per week)
- Identify 3 distribution channels for each post (where will your audience see it?)

## 6. Email Marketing and Newsletter Growth

### Growth Strategies

- **Start early**: Begin collecting emails even before your product launches (use a simple Substack, Beehiiv, or ConvertKit landing page)
- **Funnel social followers into email**: Email has 4x higher engagement than social posts
- **Cold emailing**: For bootstrapped founders, cold email can be the most effective growth channel, especially when automated with personalization
- **Cross-promotion**: Partner with complementary newsletters for mutual shout-outs
- **Content repurposing**: Turn blog posts into newsletter editions, and vice versa

### What Works

- Consistent sending schedule (same day, same time)
- Providing genuine value in every email (not just announcements)
- Segmentation based on user behavior and interests
- Welcome sequences that onboard new subscribers
- Clear, compelling subject lines

### What Doesn't Work

- Buying email lists
- Sending only when you want to sell something
- No segmentation (everyone gets the same email)
- Inconsistent sending schedule
- Overly designed emails (plain text often converts better)

### Good vs. Bad Examples

**Good**: An indie hacker running a developer tool sends a weekly newsletter with: one tip for using the product better, one industry insight, one community highlight. Open rate: 45%.

**Bad**: An indie hacker sends an email blast once every 3 months that just says "We launched a new feature! Check it out!" Open rate: 8%.

### Exercise 11: Newsletter MVP Setup (60 min)
- Sign up for a free newsletter tool (Buttondown, Substack, or Beehiiv)
- Write your first 3 newsletter editions (drafts are fine)
- Create a simple landing page with a clear value proposition for subscribing
- Set up a welcome email sequence (3 emails over 7 days)
- Draft your "About" / subscription pitch: why should someone give you their email?

### Exercise 12: Cold Email Script Workshop (45 min)
Write 3 different cold email templates:
1. For potential users (short, value-first, clear CTA)
2. For potential partners/collaborators (mutual benefit framing)
3. For press/bloggers (newsworthy angle)
For each, keep it under 100 words. Include a clear, single CTA. Personalize the opening line.

## 7. Pricing Psychology for SaaS Products

### Key Principles

- **Most indie hackers underprice by 50-70%**: Leaving significant revenue on the table
- **The Anchoring Effect**: Show a higher "original" price next to your actual price to create perceived value
- **The Goldilocks Effect**: Offer 3 tiers (not 5+). Make the middle tier the best value (this is your target tier)
- **Price transparency**: Users who can't see pricing upfront often don't sign up at all
- **Don't charge $9/month**: $9/month signals low value. Consider $29-49/month for meaningful SaaS products
- **Connect price to transformation**: Frame pricing around the value delivered, not the cost of features

### What Works

- 3 clearly differentiated pricing tiers
- Annual discount (typically 20% off) to reduce churn
- Free tier or trial to reduce friction
- Displaying prices prominently (no "contact sales" for self-serve products)
- Raising prices as you add value (existing customers grandfathered in)

### What Doesn't Work

- Racing to the bottom on price
- Too many tier options (decision paralysis)
- Hiding pricing behind a "Contact Us" wall
- Not testing prices at all
- Pricing based on costs instead of value delivered

### Good vs. Bad Examples

**Good**: Bannerbear started at $9/month, realized it signaled low value, and raised to $49/month targeting a niche audience of marketers and designers. Revenue increased without losing meaningful customers.

**Bad**: A SaaS product with 6 pricing tiers, each with 15 feature checkmarks, where customers can't tell the difference between the "Pro" and "Business" plans.

### Exercise 13: Pricing Page Analysis (45 min)
Study 5 successful SaaS products in your niche:
- Screenshot their pricing pages
- How many tiers? What's the price range?
- How do they differentiate tiers?
- What's the CTA on each tier?
- Which tier is visually highlighted (their target)?
- Design your own 3-tier pricing page based on what you learned

### Exercise 14: Value-Based Pricing Workshop (60 min)
- List 5 outcomes your product delivers (time saved, revenue generated, stress reduced)
- For each outcome, estimate the dollar value to your target user
- Calculate what 10% of that value would be (this is often your ideal price)
- Compare this to what you're currently charging
- Draft 3 pricing options and get feedback from 5 target users

## 8. App Store Optimization (ASO)

### Core Strategies

- **Keywords in title**: Strongest weight for ASO indexation on both App Store and Google Play
- **Long-tail keywords**: Less competition, more targeted users
- **Visual optimization**: Icon, screenshots, and preview video are conversion levers (not decoration)
- **Ratings matter**: Apps with 4.5+ ratings get downloaded far more often
- **Localization**: Additional locales dramatically expand reach
- **Continuous A/B testing**: Small tweaks can boost conversions significantly
- **Platform-specific**: App Store and Google Play optimize differently

### What Works

- Keyword-rich app title that clearly describes what the app does
- Screenshots that tell a story (show the user journey, not just feature screenshots)
- Video preview showing the app in action
- Prompting happy users for reviews at the right moment (after a positive experience)
- Regular updates that show active development

### What Doesn't Work

- Generic app names that don't communicate value
- Screenshots that are just raw app screens without context or captions
- Ignoring ratings and reviews
- "Set and forget" approach to the app store listing
- Same optimization strategy for iOS and Android

### Good vs. Bad Examples

**Good**: A productivity app called "Focus Timer - Pomodoro Study Aid" with screenshots showing before/after scenarios of distracted vs. focused work sessions, a 4.7 rating, and localization in 15 languages.

**Bad**: An app called "FocusPro" with no subtitle, screenshots that are just raw UI screenshots without captions, a 3.2 rating with no developer responses to reviews.

### Exercise 15: ASO Audit (45 min)
For your app (or a competitor's):
- Check keyword density in title and subtitle
- Evaluate all screenshots -- do they tell a story?
- Read the last 20 reviews -- what do users love/hate?
- Compare your listing side-by-side with the top 3 competitors
- List 5 specific improvements you'd make

## 9. Growth Hacking for Bootstrapped Products

### Effective Techniques

- **Product-led growth**: Design features that encourage users to invite others (collaborative features, shared dashboards, "powered by" badges)
- **Micro-communities**: Build/join specialized spaces (private Slack groups, niche forums) where engagement is deep
- **Build in public**: Share your journey openly to attract early supporters
- **Intent-based SEO**: Target precise queries that align with actual buying behavior
- **Free tools as lead magnets**: Build small free tools that solve a specific problem and funnel users to your main product
- **Integrations**: Build integrations with popular tools to tap into their user base

### What Works

- Offering genuine value before asking for anything
- Leveraging AI tools for cost-effective content and outreach
- Charging early and iterating with customer feedback
- Focus on organic channels before paid
- Creative approaches that don't require large budgets

### What Doesn't Work

- Copying VC-funded growth playbooks (hiring 10 sales reps) on a bootstrapped budget
- Spammy tactics that burn your reputation
- Relying on a single channel
- Premature scaling before product-market fit
- Ignoring retention while chasing acquisition

### Good vs. Bad Examples

**Good**: Tally (free form builder) grew by embedding a small "Made with Tally" badge on free-tier forms. Every form respondent saw the badge, creating a viral loop with zero ad spend.

**Bad**: A founder spending $2,000/month on Facebook ads before validating that anyone wants their product, getting 10,000 visitors but only 2 signups.

### Exercise 16: Growth Channel Brainstorm (60 min)
- List every possible channel for reaching your target users (aim for 20+)
- Score each channel: effort (1-5), cost (1-5), potential reach (1-5), timeline (short/medium/long)
- Pick the top 3 highest-potential, lowest-cost channels
- Create a 2-week experiment for each (measurable goal, specific actions, success criteria)

## 10. Analytics and Metrics That Matter

### Vanity Metrics (Avoid Obsessing Over)

- Total page views or website visitors
- Social media followers count
- Total registered users (without context)
- Total downloads
- Raw email subscriber count

### Actionable Metrics (Track These)

- **MRR (Monthly Recurring Revenue)**: Your actual income
- **Churn rate**: Percentage of customers leaving per month (aim for under 5%)
- **Customer Lifetime Value (CLTV)**: Revenue generated per customer over their lifetime
- **Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)**: Cost to acquire each new customer
- **CLTV:CAC ratio**: Should be at least 3:1
- **Trial-to-paid conversion rate**: How many free users become paying customers
- **Daily/Weekly Active Users**: More meaningful than total signups
- **Net Revenue Retention (NRR)**: Revenue from existing customers including expansion minus churn

### Good vs. Bad Examples

**Good**: An indie hacker tracks weekly active users, trial-to-paid conversion rate, and churn rate. They notice that users who complete onboarding within 24 hours have 3x higher retention, so they focus on improving the first-day experience.

**Bad**: An indie hacker celebrates "10,000 signups!" but doesn't track that only 200 are monthly active users and churn is 15% per month. They keep focusing on acquisition instead of fixing the leaky bucket.

### Exercise 17: Metrics Dashboard Setup (60 min)
- Set up a simple analytics dashboard (use a free tool like Plausible, PostHog, or even a spreadsheet)
- Define your 5 most important metrics
- For each metric, set a target and a "red flag" threshold
- Create a weekly review habit: every Monday, spend 15 minutes reviewing your dashboard
- Write down what one number tells you to do differently this week

## 11. User Retention Strategies

### Core Principles

- **Onboarding is everything**: Up to 75% of users churn within the first week if onboarding is poor
- **Time-to-Value (TTV)**: Get users to their "aha moment" within the first session, ideally under 5 minutes
- **Retention beats acquisition**: Once past initial traction, retention is more important than getting new users
- **Personalization**: Tailor onboarding to different user roles and goals
- **Proactive support**: Companies with proactive onboarding support see 2x higher net revenue retention

### What Works

- Interactive walkthroughs and checklists that guide users to their first success
- Email onboarding sequences triggered by user behavior
- Celebrating user milestones (first task completed, first week, etc.)
- Regular product improvements based on user feedback
- Re-engagement campaigns for users who go dormant

### What Doesn't Work

- Dumping users into a complex interface with no guidance
- Sending the same onboarding emails to all users regardless of their goals
- Only reaching out when you want to upsell
- Ignoring churn signals (decreased usage, support complaints)
- Focusing only on acquiring new users while existing ones leave

### Good vs. Bad Examples

**Good**: Notion sends a series of onboarding emails based on what template you chose when signing up. If you picked "Project Management," your emails focus on kanban boards and team collaboration features.

**Bad**: A SaaS product drops you into a blank dashboard after signup with a generic "Welcome! Explore our features" message and no guidance.

### Exercise 18: Onboarding Flow Mapping (60 min)
- Map your current onboarding flow step by step (from signup to first value)
- Time each step -- how long does it take?
- Identify the "aha moment" -- the point where users realize the product's value
- Find the biggest drop-off point (where most users abandon)
- Redesign the flow to reach the aha moment in under 5 minutes
- Draft 3 onboarding emails that guide users through the critical first week

## 12. Community Building Around Products

### Building a Community

- Start small and focused (10-50 engaged members beats 1,000 lurkers)
- Create ongoing activities: monthly events, challenges, AMAs, member spotlights
- Require participation (communities with engagement requirements stay alive longer)
- Use 1-1 matching to introduce members to each other based on shared interests
- Automate welcome messages and nudges while keeping the brand voice authentic

### Platform Considerations

- **Discord**: Good for real-time chat, especially for developer/gaming communities
- **Slack**: Better for professional/B2B communities, but free tier has message limits
- **Circle/Discourse**: Better for async discussions and content organization
- **GitHub Discussions**: Natural for open-source projects

### Good vs. Bad Examples

**Good**: A community that starts with free monthly Zoom calls, adds a Slack group for conversations, creates a 1-1 matching system to connect founders with similar challenges, and runs monthly challenges.

**Bad**: A founder creates a Discord server, posts a welcome message, and then... nothing. No ongoing activities, no structure, no reason for members to return. The server dies within 2 months.

### Exercise 19: Community MVP Design (45 min)
Design a minimal community for your product:
- Who are the first 20 members? (List specific people/roles you'd invite)
- What platform will you use and why?
- What's the weekly/monthly rhythm? (events, challenges, discussions)
- What value do members get that they can't get elsewhere?
- What's the one rule that will keep the community healthy?
- Write the welcome message for new members

## 13. Cold Outreach and Customer Development

### Getting Your First Customers

The very first customers are ALL earned through direct conversations: cold calls, friends of friends, direct meetings, manual work. There is no shortcut.

### Customer Discovery Interviews

- Reach out to 100 potential users on LinkedIn; expect 5-10 to agree to a 15-minute call
- Use "The Mom Test" framework: ask about their problems, not whether they'd use your product
- Ask about their current workarounds and what they're already paying for
- Listen for emotional language -- that's where the real pain is

### Cold Outreach That Works

- Personalized first line that shows you understand their specific situation
- Under 100 words
- Single, clear call-to-action
- Focus on THEIR problem, not YOUR product
- Follow up 2-3 times (most responses come on follow-up #2 or #3)

### Good vs. Bad Examples

**Good Cold Email**: "Hi [Name], I noticed you mentioned on [Forum] that you spend 3+ hours weekly on [specific task]. I built a tool that automates this for [specific role]. Would you be open to a 10-minute demo? Either way, I'd love to hear how you currently handle this."

**Bad Cold Email**: "Hi! I'm excited to introduce you to [Product Name], the revolutionary platform that leverages AI-powered insights to transform your workflow. Our enterprise-grade solution features..." (Too long, too generic, too self-focused)

### Exercise 20: Customer Interview Challenge (90 min)
- Identify 20 potential users of your product (or idea) on LinkedIn, Twitter, or relevant forums
- Craft a personalized outreach message for each (max 3 sentences)
- Send all 20 messages
- For those who respond, conduct a 15-minute interview using The Mom Test questions:
  - "Tell me about the last time you dealt with [problem]"
  - "What did you do about it?"
  - "What else have you tried?"
  - "How much does this problem cost you (time/money)?"
  - "What would your ideal solution look like?"

### Exercise 21: Cold Email A/B Test (45 min)
Write two versions of a cold outreach email:
- Version A: Leads with the pain point
- Version B: Leads with a specific result/outcome
- Send each to 10 people
- Track open rates and response rates
- Analyze which approach resonated better and why

## 14. Positioning and Messaging Frameworks

### April Dunford's "Obviously Awesome" Framework

Positioning defines how your product is different and better than alternatives for a particular set of customers. It consists of 5 components:

1. **Competitive Alternatives**: What would customers use if your product didn't exist?
2. **Unique Attributes**: What features/capabilities do you have that alternatives don't?
3. **Value**: What benefit do those unique attributes deliver?
4. **Target Customer**: Who cares the most about that value?
5. **Market Category**: What context makes your value obvious?

### Three Styles of Positioning

1. **Head-to-head**: Position directly against the market leader (requires clear differentiation)
2. **Big fish, small pond**: Dominate a niche subsegment of a larger market
3. **Create a new category**: Define an entirely new market category (hardest, highest reward)

### Good vs. Bad Examples

**Good**: "Plausible: Simple and privacy-friendly Google Analytics alternative" -- Clear competitive alternative (Google Analytics), unique attributes (simple, privacy-friendly), target customer (privacy-conscious website owners), market category (analytics).

**Bad**: "The #1 AI-Powered Enterprise Solutions Platform" -- No competitive alternative, no unique attributes, no target customer, vague market category.

### Exercise 22: Positioning Canvas Workshop (60 min)
Fill out this positioning canvas for your product:
1. List 5 competitive alternatives your customers currently use
2. For each, list what you do that they don't (unique attributes)
3. Translate each unique attribute into a customer benefit
4. Describe who cares most about these benefits (be specific -- job title, company size, situation)
5. What market category makes your positioning obvious?
6. Write a one-sentence positioning statement: "[Product] is [market category] that [key differentiator] for [target customer]"

## 15. Viral Loops and Referral Programs

### How Viral Loops Work

A viral loop is: User discovers product -> User gets value -> User shares with others -> New users discover product -> Repeat. Products with viral coefficients above 1.0 achieve exponential growth.

### Effective Referral Strategies

- **Dual-sided rewards**: Both referrer and referee benefit (Dropbox gave 500MB to both)
- **Wait for the aha moment**: Trigger referral prompts after users experience core value
- **Make sharing frictionless**: One-click referral links, pre-written messages
- **Built-in virality**: "Powered by [Product]" badges, shared dashboards, collaborative features
- **Track the K-factor**: (number of invites per user) x (conversion rate of invites)

### Real-World Examples

- **Dropbox**: 500MB free storage for both referrer and new user
- **PayPal**: $10 for signup + $10 for each referral (early growth phase)
- **Notion**: Extra AI credits for each referred friend
- **Trello**: Free month of Trello Gold per referral (up to 12 months free)
- **Spotify**: Both parties get 1 free month of premium

### Exercise 23: Design Your Viral Loop (60 min)
- Map the core user journey of your product
- Identify the natural "share moment" -- where would a user organically want to tell someone?
- Design a referral mechanism:
  - What does the referrer get?
  - What does the new user get?
  - How many steps to share? (aim for 1-2)
- Calculate your hypothetical K-factor
- Sketch the full viral loop on paper

## 16. Competitor Analysis Frameworks

### The Analysis Process

1. **Identify competitors** (direct, indirect, and potential alternatives -- aim for fewer than 10)
2. **Analyze their product**: Features, pricing, UX, customer reviews
3. **Analyze their marketing**: Channels, messaging, content strategy, social presence
4. **SWOT analysis**: Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats for each
5. **Identify gaps**: What are they NOT doing that your customers need?

### Key Areas to Analyze

- Feature comparison (what's missing, what's overdone)
- Pricing model and tiers
- Customer reviews (what do users love and hate about them?)
- Marketing channels and content strategy
- Technical architecture (if relevant)
- Team size and funding (to understand their capacity)

### Good vs. Bad Examples

**Good**: An indie hacker reads 100 negative reviews of the leading competitor on G2, identifies 3 consistent complaints, and builds a product that specifically solves those 3 problems.

**Bad**: An indie hacker clones a competitor's feature list without understanding WHY those features exist or what customers actually want.

## 17. Building in Public

### The Strategy

Building in public means sharing your entrepreneurial journey openly -- the wins, losses, and behind-the-scenes details. It builds trust, attracts early supporters, and creates accountability.

### What to Share

- Revenue milestones and metrics (with context)
- Technical challenges and how you solved them
- Customer feedback (anonymized) and how it shaped the product
- Failed experiments and what you learned
- Weekly/monthly progress updates

### The 2025 Shift

Many successful indie hackers are becoming more strategic about what they share. Some are going dark to protect their business from copycats. The key is finding the balance between transparency and protection.

### What Works

- Consistent posting schedule (daily or weekly updates)
- Being genuinely helpful to others, not just self-promotional
- Sharing both wins AND failures (failures often get more engagement)
- Engaging with others' journeys, not just broadcasting your own
- Projects shared publicly have 30% higher community engagement than private ones

### What Doesn't Work

- Only sharing wins (feels inauthentic)
- Over-sharing sensitive business information that competitors can exploit
- Treating it as a marketing tactic rather than genuine transparency
- Inconsistent posting (a burst of updates followed by months of silence)

### Good vs. Bad Examples

**Good**: @levelsio (Pieter Levels) shares monthly revenue updates, technical decisions, and honest reflections on what worked and what didn't. His transparency helped build a following of 400K+ on Twitter.

**Bad**: A founder shares "Just hit $10K MRR!" with no context, no journey, no lessons -- just a flex. Or the opposite: sharing so much detail that three competitors clone their approach within weeks.

## Summary: The Vibe Coder Marketing Mindset

Marketing "taste" for vibe coders comes down to these principles:

1. **Talk to users, not at them**: Every marketing decision should start with understanding your user's actual words, problems, and desires
2. **Be specific, not generic**: "Save 3 hours per week on invoice management" beats "Increase productivity" every time
3. **Show, don't tell**: Screenshots, demos, case studies, and real numbers are more convincing than adjectives
4. **Consistency beats intensity**: One hour of marketing daily beats a 10-hour marketing binge once a month
5. **Measure what matters**: Track actionable metrics, not vanity metrics
6. **Start before you're ready**: The best landing page is the one that's live, not the one that's perfect in your head
7. **Steal from the best**: Study what works for others, adapt it to your context, and iterate based on your results
8. **Price for value, not for cost**: Your customers care about the outcome, not your server bills
9. **Build in public, but build strategically**: Share your journey, but protect your competitive advantages
10. **Marketing is product development**: Every customer conversation, every landing page test, every piece of feedback is making your product better
